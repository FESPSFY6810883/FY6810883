Abstract

Membrane filtration is one of the critical methods in the modern treatment of drinking water, offering solutions to the purification of water, and making it safe for consumption. This paper examines 4 membrane filtration processes: microfiltration, ultrafiltration, nanofiltration, and reverse osmosis. Each of these methods is presented with the capability of what it does: microfiltration and ultrafiltration, larger particles and microorganisms; nanofiltration and reverse osmosis, smaller contaminants like salts, heavy metals, and dissolved organic matter. Applications for such filtration methods are also discussed to outline their applicability in water purification. Also, the challenges regarding membrane fouling and energy requirements with reverse osmosis are discussed. The newest developments that focus on the improvement of membrane materials, and thus on the future possibilities of membrane filtration as a unit operation in providing safe drinking water, will be discussed.

Introduction

Clean drinking water is a basic right of every human being, the quality of which bears immense importance to public health. The technology of water treatment has evolved with time to tackle the rising risks of contamination. Among such methods, membrane filtration has emerged as one of the most effective and widely used methods for purifying drinking water. This technology uses the principle of selective permeability, involving mainly the separation of contaminants from water with the use of membranes based on the size of the particles as well as other properties. Microfiltration, ultrafiltration, nanofiltration, and reverse osmosis—these four big categories serve at different levels in water filtration with each having some unique advantages and limitations. The paper describes these technologies, their use, the challenges, and the novelties which set a trend in modern water treatment systems.

Types of Membrane Filtration

Microfiltration (MF) — removes larger-sized particles (sediments, bacteria, protozoa). Pore size ~0.1–10 μm; often used as pre‑treatment; does not address dissolved contaminants or smaller microorganisms like viruses (Schafer, 2018).

Ultrafiltration (UF) — pore size ~0.01–0.1 μm; filters viruses, bacteria and larger dissolved organic molecules; useful for high-microbial surface water; does not remove salts or dissolved inorganics (Hassan, 2017).

Nanofiltration (NF) — ~0.001–0.01 μm; removes divalent ions (e.g., Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺), some organics and pathogens; used for softening and targeted contaminant removal (Singh, 2019).

Reverse osmosis (RO) — ~0.0001 μm; removes most dissolved solids, heavy metals and organics; widely used for desalination; drawbacks include energy intensity and fouling (Miller, 2021).

Applications in Drinking Water Treatment

Each membrane filtration type has a specific role, and systems often combine them as a multi‑barrier approach. MF and UF remove larger particles/pathogens and protect downstream processes. NF reduces hardness and removes selected contaminants while retaining beneficial minerals. RO is used for desalination and near‑complete removal of dissolved contaminants where freshwater is scarce (Miller, 2021).

Challenges and Innovations

Membrane fouling (accumulation on the membrane surface) reduces efficiency and increases energy/operational cost. Research targets fouling‑resistant materials and improved cleaning strategies (Schafer 2018). For RO, energy demand is a key constraint; recent work focuses on energy‑recovery systems and more permeable, selective membranes (Singh 2019).

Conclusion

Membrane filtration is a major modern function in drinking‑water treatment. From MF for particulate removal to RO for desalination, each technology contributes to safe, reliable supply. Though fouling and energy consumption are the main challenges, continuous developments in materials and system efficiencies are promising for more sustainable, cost‑efficient solutions.
