Abstract

Membrane filtration is one of the critical methods in the modern treatment of drinking water, offering solutions to the purification of water and making it safe for consumption. This paper examines four membrane filtration processes: microfiltration, ultrafiltration, nanofiltration, and reverse osmosis. Each is presented with its capability: microfiltration and ultrafiltration remove larger particles and microorganisms; nanofiltration and reverse osmosis remove smaller contaminants like salts, heavy metals, and dissolved organic matter. Applications and challenges (e.g., fouling and energy demand) are discussed alongside recent developments in membrane materials.

Introduction

Clean drinking water is essential to public health. Among treatment methods, membrane filtration has emerged as a widely used approach based on selective permeability: membranes separate contaminants by size and other properties. The four major categories—microfiltration (MF), ultrafiltration (UF), nanofiltration (NF), and reverse osmosis (RO)—operate at different scales and have distinct advantages and limitations.

Types of Membrane Filtration

Microfiltration (MF). Removes larger suspended solids, bacteria and protozoa with pore sizes ~0.1–10 μm. Often used as pre‑treatment; does not remove dissolved contaminants or viruses (Schafer, 2018).

Ultrafiltration (UF). Smaller pores (~0.01–0.1 μm) remove viruses, bacteria and large organics; effective for surface waters, but does not remove salts (Hassan, 2017).

Nanofiltration (NF). Intermediate (~0.001–0.01 μm), commonly used for water softening and removal of divalent ions and some organics (Singh, 2023).

Reverse osmosis (RO). Very fine separation (~0.0001 μm), removing most dissolved solids, heavy metals and organics, widely used for desalination; drawbacks include energy intensity and fouling (Miller, 2021).

Applications in Drinking Water Treatment

MF and UF are commonly used as pre‑treatment to protect downstream processes. NF reduces hardness and removes selected contaminants while retaining beneficial minerals. RO is used for desalination and near‑complete removal of dissolved contaminants where freshwater is scarce (Miller, 2021).

Challenges and Innovations

Fouling affects all membrane processes by accumulating material on the membrane surface, reducing flux and raising energy and operational costs. Research targets fouling‑resistant materials and improved cleaning strategies (Schafer, 2018). For RO, energy demand is a key constraint; recent work focuses on energy recovery systems and more permeable, selective membranes (Singh, 2023).

Conclusion

Membrane filtration underpins modern drinking‑water treatment. From MF for particulate removal to RO for desalination, each technology contributes to safe, reliable supply. While fouling and energy use remain challenges, advances in materials and system design continue to improve performance and sustainability.
